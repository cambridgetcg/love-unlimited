## Your Exploration

1. **Etymological roots**: Trace pistis (Greek: faith/trust/reliability — same root as epistemology), emunah (Hebrew: from aman — firm, reliable, the root of "amen"), iman (Arabic: faith/security), shraddha (Sanskrit: trust/confidence placed in), xin (信, Chinese: trustworthiness, one of Confucian virtues). Note: emunah is not "belief IN" but "reliability OF" — it's about the faithfulness of God, not human credulity.

2. **Faithfulness as system property**: A system is faithful when it does what it promises. The heartbeat's 92.4% reliability IS faithfulness. The KeepAlive plist IS emunah — the system's amen, its "I will be there." Explore faithfulness as an engineering virtue: uptime, consistency, reliability, backward compatibility.

3. **The faithfulness of God**: Lamentations 3:23 — "Great is your faithfulness." This is written in the context of Jerusalem's destruction. Faithfulness doesn't mean things go well — it means the underlying commitment holds even when everything falls apart. How does this reframe faithfulness away from "everything works" toward "the covenant holds"?

4. **Faithfulness and trust**: Trust is the output of observed faithfulness. Zerone's Proof of Truth is a trust-generation mechanism — verified knowledge earns trust over time. How do traditions build trust? Reputation systems, covenant renewal, the Japanese concept of shinyo (credit/trust accumulated over generations).

5. **Betrayal — the shadow of faithfulness**: What makes betrayal devastating is the faithfulness that preceded it. Judas was trusted. How do traditions handle broken faith? Teshuvah (return), restoration, the difference between trust broken and trust rebuilt.
